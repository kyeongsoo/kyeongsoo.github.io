MULTI-CHANNEL SCHEDULING WITH TUNABLE TRANSCEIVERS IN HYBRID TDM/WDM OPTICAL NETWORKS

Sponsor: Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council (EPSRC)

Duration: 01/10/2009 - 31/03/2013

Summary:

There has been observed a fundamental shift in optical networking paradigms: The focus in network architectures is put on the transition from static to dynamic-reconfigurable networks; many new network architectures are based on passive or semi-passive components, which replace active components and systems in the field and thereby greatly reduce maintenance costs; they are also based on shared network resources rather than dedicated ones; the design of new optical network architectures is now driven not only by performance but also by energy efficiency.

Major driving forces behind this shift are the mismatch between current service/usage models and static network infrastructures, and the unbalance in bandwidth between backbone and access networks. The fixed and static nature of the current optical networks makes difficult fast provisioning and cannot meet well the varying user demands, which leads into inefficient use of network resources and thereby results in poor energy efficiency. Next-generation optical networks are trying to address these issues by providing fast and easy reconfigurability through network architectures based on tunable and passive components.

These changes in the network design specifically focuses on the way of using wavelengths. The traditional way is through WDM in a point-to-point link and continuous-mode communications mainly to increase the link capacity. The complexity and power consumption of the switches/routers in central nodes in this case, however, would be extremely high due to the need of very fast processing, switching, and buffering mostly done in the electrical domain. On the other hand, many next-generation architectures replace the high-capacity switches/routers with distributed use of tunable lasers and/or burst-mode, fixed receivers with wavelength-selective passive or semi-passive intermediate nodes, trying to efficiently assign and use the available network resources, which results in higher energy efficiency as well.

The use of tunable transceivers has many advantages: It enables network resource sharing in both time and wavelength domains. With properly designed scheduling algorithms, it could provide virtually the same performance with a smaller number of transceivers compared to traditional WDM. Also, by dynamically reassigning network resources and turning off unnecessary transceivers during inactive periods, it can increase energy efficiency. As for network protection and restoration, a pool of tunable transceivers could still maintain normal operation without interruption when some of them fail.

There have been several works on multi-channel scheduling with tunable transceivers in optical networks. Most of the works, however, are specific to network architectures under consideration and, because the major focus is on the delay and throughput performance of the whole system, there is no support for fairness and QoS guarantee in the flow level.

To make further progress in the area of multi-channel scheduling with tunable transceivers, we propose to develop a general theoretical framework providing performance bounds and guiding principles for the design of scheduling algorithms in hybrid TDM/WDM optical networks with the following objectives: 1) To extend the Fair Queueing (FQ) framework to the case of multiple channels with tunable transmitters; 2) to design and evaluate the performance of schedulers for hybrid TDM/WDM Passive Optical Networks (PONs) providing fairness and QoS guarantee in the flow level.

The outcomes from the proposed work could be directly applicable to future hybrid TDM/WDM metro and access networks. Also, the scheduling algorithms and their theoretical framework could be generalised and applied to scheduling in other types of networks (e.g., WLANs).
